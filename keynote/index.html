Dr. Martina Morris

Professor of Sociology and Statistics at the University of Washington

Dr. Martina Morris a sociologist with interests in the analysis of social structure and population dynamics. She received her Ph.D. in Sociology from the University of Chicago in 1989. She holds a joint appointment as professor in the departments of Sociology and Statistics at the University of Washington, and is the founding director of the Sociobehavioral and Prevention Research Core in the UW CFAR (Center for Aids Research). Her work focuses on the development of statistical methodology for network analysis, applications to modeling epidemics on networks, and developing local models for HIV prevention planning.

Her research is interdisciplinary, intersecting with demography, economics, epidemiology and public health, and statistics. Examples from her current projects include the study of partnership networks in the spread of HIV/AIDS, the impact of economic restructuring on inequality and mobility, and the development of Relative Distribution methods for statistical analysis. She joined the faculty at the University of Washington in September 2000.

At the Center for Aids Research (CFAR), she serves as the Director for the Sociobehavioral and Prevention Research Core.

Talk title: From AI to AI: Acquiring Social Media Intelligence via "Big" Data

Abstract: As the old adage goes, “knowledge is power”. With the pervasive presence of big data, data is a new source of power. Social media data is mainly user-generated with geo-spatial, pictorial, temporal, and social information. We use social media data to acquire intelligence about user behavior, user needs, sentiment, opinions, and trends. This presentation examines issues arising from social media intelligence acquisition: why data is power, whether big data is really big, how we can solve problems or verify hypotheses with social media data, how we can obtain interpretable machine-extracted topics, and how we can figure out what we don’t know. With adaptive and evolving bots, social media intelligence could be obfuscated with rampant misinformation, thus it is challenging to acquire accurate social media intelligence. With the joint force of AI and social media data, we hope to develop novel algorithms for acquiring social media intelligence with collaborative research.
